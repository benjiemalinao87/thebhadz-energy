§ 4Packaging & brand

  • Named packages, not kilowatts. LIWANAG (light), ILAW (light/lamp), SANDIGAN (support/backbone) — Filipino words a homeowner remembers and repeats. The kWp spec lives in the fine print; the promise lives in the name.
  • Physical packaging that signals quality: panels and our built components arrive on a branded pallet with corner protection (SC-04 §4); the power box, harness, and racking wear Solar City labels; the owner gets a printed handover pack — system spec, wiring map, warranty card, monitoring login, and a one-page "what to do in a typhoon."
  • Warranty as a headline, not a footnote: match the market — 10–12 yr on our built hardware and workmanship, pass-through of panel (25–30 yr performance) and inverter warranties, and — our edge — a local company that answers the phone and ships a single replacement panel (importers won't).
  • Proof over adjectives: every install is a case study — before/after bill, photos, a short testimonial, a map pin. Solar in the Philippines sells house-to-house and barangay-to-barangay; a visible neighbor's system outsells any ad.

§ 6Feasibility check — is ₱80–100k real?

Yes, at residential essential-load scale. The SC-05 §4 cost stack lands the on-grid flagship at ₱62–86k cost against a ₱99,500 price (14–38% gross), and the off-grid flagship works if the battery is held to 2.5–3 kWh — a bigger battery is the one thing that breaks the price point, which is exactly why storage is an upsell, not a default. What ₱80–100k does not buy: a whole-home off-grid system, large batteries, or 5 kWp+ arrays — those are the PLUS tier and up, and honest sizing at the door protects both the customer and our margin.
