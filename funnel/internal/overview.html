SC-00 · OVERVIEW · REV A · 2026-07-06

Bare cells in. 400 W modules out.

This file is the working manual for designing, hand-building, and then mass-producing our own crystalline-silicon solar modules in the Philippines. We do not fabricate cells — nobody outside a wafer fab does. We buy cells and engineer everything after that: the module, the process, the line, and the route to market.

§ 1The route

Three stages, each one de-risking the next. Do not skip a stage: every later stage reuses the process knowledge, supplier relationships, and test data from the one before it.

Stage 1 · Now

Hand-built prototypes

Build 3–5 modules on a bench: tab, string, lay up, laminate, frame, test. Goal is process literacy — knowing exactly why each layer, temperature, and tolerance exists.

QTY: 3–5 modules
CAPEX: benchtop scale
EXIT: a module that survives testing
DOCS: SC-01 → SC-02 → SC-03
Stage 2

Small-batch workshop

A workshop line — laminator, stringer or trained tabbing stations, EL and flash test — producing tens to hundreds of modules per month for pilot customers and certification units.

QTY: 50–500 modules/mo
CAPEX: workshop line
EXIT: IEC 61215/61730 certificate
DOCS: SC-03 → SC-04
Stage 3

Production line

A registered manufacturing operation with an automated line, certified module family, and a distribution strategy that survives contact with imported-module pricing.

QTY: 10–50 MW/yr
CAPEX: full line + facility
EXIT: sustained sell-through
DOCS: SC-04

§ 4Ground rules

  • Scope. We are a module assembler, not a cell fab. Cell fabrication is a multi-hundred-million-dollar semiconductor process; module assembly is precision manufacturing you can start on a workbench. Every serious module company outside China started exactly here.
  • Target product. Standard residential module: ~400 W, 108 half-cut M10 monocrystalline cells, glass front, aluminum frame. The build guide starts you on a smaller 36-cell trainer first — cheap mistakes before expensive ones.
  • Numbers are dated. Cell prices, freight rates, and equipment quotes move monthly. Every figure in SC-03/SC-04 carries an as-of date; anything we could not verify is marked UNVERIFIED. Re-quote before committing money.
  • Safety. Lamination runs at ~150 °C, hi-pot testing at up to 3 kV DC, and a lit module string is a current source that does not care about your multimeter's fuse. Safety callouts in SC-02 are process steps, not suggestions.
