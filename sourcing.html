SC-05 · SOURCE & INSTALL · REV A · 2026-07-06

Model B: buy the hard parts, build the rest, install everything

The alternative to manufacturing modules (SC-01…04 = Model A): source the components that are uneconomical to reproduce — panels, inverters, batteries — fabricate everything we credibly can, and make our money as the system builder and installer. This model earns revenue in weeks, not years, and later becomes the sales channel for our own modules.

How A and B fit together Model B is not a retreat from Model A — it is stage 0 revenue. Installing sourced panels teaches us the customer, the roof, the permitting process, and the service economics, and builds the distribution channel that a factory would otherwise have to buy. When Model A reaches stage 2 (certified modules), our own panels drop into an install pipeline that already exists.

§ 1Buy vs build — the honest split

Rails, roof hooks, tilt frames, ground mounts — cut, drilled, hot-dip galvanized steel or anodized aluminum from Philippine fabricators at a fraction of imported racking prices, sized for local roof types (GI sheet, tile, concrete deck) and wind zones.

Monitoring BUY + BRAND Use the inverter vendor's platform; wrap it in our onboarding and alerts. Custom software later, if ever.

§ 2Sourcing channels for the bought parts

  • Start (installs 1–20): buy panels/inverters/batteries from Metro Manila solar distributors and importers — pay the middleman margin in exchange for zero inventory risk, local warranty handling, and next-day availability. Get dealer accounts with 2–3 distributors and make them compete per order.
  • Scale (20+ installs/quarter): direct import — one 20-ft container holds roughly 300–360 panels (~130–150 kWp), enough for 50–70 residential systems. This is where per-watt cost drops 20–35% EST and the SC-04 §4 logistics playbook (freight, packaging, breakage accounting) starts applying to us as the importer.
  • Selection rules: only IEC 61215/61730-certified modules from makers with a PH warranty presence; only inverters on the DU's approved/net-metering-friendly lists; LiFePO₄ only, with BMS documentation. Every SKU we standardize on halves our training and spares problem.

§ 3The install operation

Residential install crew — steady-state
RoleCountNotes
Licensed electrician / lead1Owns electrical work, testing, sign-off prep
Installers / roof crew2Racking, panels, harness routing
Registered Electrical Engineer (PEE/REE)retainerPlan signing for permits and net-metering applications
Sales / site assessor1Roof survey, bill analysis, quote (see SC-06)
  • Throughput: a trained crew completes a 2–3 kWp residential install in 1–2 days; one crew ≈ 10–14 installs/month sustained.
  • Process per job: site survey + bill analysis → design (panel layout, string sizing — reuse SC-01 thinking) → quote → electrical permit (LGU, REE-signed plans) → install → testing (insulation, polarity, torque map) → DU net-metering application (on-grid) or commissioning + owner training (off-grid) → handover pack.
  • Net metering (on-grid): RA 9513's net-metering program covers systems up to 100 kW; the distribution utility (e.g. Meralco) processes the application, inspects, and swaps the meter. Budget 4–12 weeks and make the paperwork our job, not the customer's — it is a core part of what they pay for. Exported energy is credited at the DU's blended generation rate (lower than retail), so systems are sized for self-consumption first.
  • Safety/compliance: Philippine Electrical Code throughout; typhoon-rated mounting (our racking's selling point); anti-islanding verified on every grid-tie commissioning.

§ 5What "we build the rest" makes true

  • Differentiation an installer can't fake: our own typhoon-rated racking, tested harnesses, and branded power boxes make the quote physically different from the three other quotes the homeowner collected.
  • Margin capture: fabricated racking + assembled boxes move ~₱10–14k per install from a supplier's pocket into ours.
  • A product ladder: racking and power boxes are sellable wholesale to other installers — a second revenue line that costs us nothing extra to develop.
  • The bridge to Model A: every install is a future retrofit/expansion customer for Solar City-branded modules the day our stage-2 line is certified.
Decision framing Model B first, Model A in parallel at prototype scale. Kill neither: B pays the bills and builds the channel; A builds the moat. Revisit the split when B reaches ~20 installs/month or A reaches certification — whichever comes first.
