For a domestic-market module assembler the RE Act route is usually the stronger package (7-yr ITH regardless of tier/location + VAT-free inputs), at the cost of DOE accreditation paperwork and per-shipment endorsements. Decide with counsel at stage-2 entry; nothing is needed for stage-1 prototyping beyond ordinary imports.

3.2 BOI vs PEZA

  • BOI is the default for a domestic or mixed-market manufacturer. Process: SEC/DTI registration → BOI Form 501 + project study → evaluation → Board approval; typically 10–20 working days after complete filing, deemed approved if not acted on in 20; registration fee ≈ 0.1% of project cost. Projects ≤ ₱15B are approved at IPA level (post-CREATE MORE).
  • PEZA only fits exporters: ecozones are outside customs territory, so every module sold into the Philippine market from a zone bears duty + 12% VAT, with domestic sales capped around 30%. If the plan is PH rooftops, PEZA is the wrong tool.
  • Foreign ownership (if you take foreign investors): 100% foreign equity is allowed for manufacturing; a >40% foreign-owned domestic-market enterprise needs US$200k paid-in capital, reducible to US$100k with ≥15 Filipino direct employees or DOST-certified advanced technology.
  • Also required regardless: mayor's permit, BIR registration, and — for a plant — DENR environmental compliance (ECC or Certificate of Non-Coverage; a small assembly workshop typically starts as non-covered).

3.3 Certification to sell

  • IEC 61215 (design qualification) + IEC 61730 (safety) are the passport — required by serious buyers, financiers, and for export. Testing consumes ~15–30 modules and runs through labs with SEA presence (TÜV Rheinland, TÜV SÜD, SGS, Intertek, UL Solutions). Budget $30k–80k and 6–12 months EST per module family — one reason to certify once, at stage 2, on a frozen design.
  • Philippine market: confirm current Bureau of Philippine Standards treatment of PV modules (mandatory ICC/PS coverage has historically applied to other electrical products; PV module status needs a direct BPS check UNVERIFIED) and DOE accreditation requirements for net-metering equipment lists.
  • Warranty to match the market: 12-yr product / 25–30-yr performance (≥ ~87% at year 25/30) is the imported-module standard your brand will be compared against.

§ 4Logistics

  • Inbound: consolidate China-side via one forwarder; LCL sea to Manila for sheet goods and frames, air for cells at prototype scale. At stage 2+, inputs arrive FCL and the RE-Act/BOI duty treatment (§3) applies. Rule: never let one late component idle the laminator — hold 4–6 weeks of input buffer once you have a line.
  • Packaging finished modules: vertical on-edge in pallet boxes, ~26–36 modules per pallet, corner protectors, strap-not-shrink. Design the pallet before designing the truck route.
  • Inter-island distribution (Visayas/Mindanao) via RoRo/domestic container adds cost and breakage risk — launch in Luzon within trucking distance of the plant; expand island by island only with a local partner who owns last-mile handling.
  • Breakage accounting: track it as a named cost line from day one (imported-module distributors quote ~0.5–2% EST); your warranty terms must exclude transit damage after handover.

§ 5The competitive math — read before stage 3

The uncomfortable number Tier-1 Chinese modules have sold in the ~$0.09–0.13/W FOB range through 2025–26 EST. Your batch-scale materials alone are ~$0.12–0.18/W (SC-03 §4) before labor, yield loss, capex, and certification. A small Philippine assembler will not win on price per watt against imports. The plan must win somewhere else.

Where a local assembler can actually win:

  • Niche formats imports don't serve: non-standard sizes for jeepney/tricycle/marine and off-grid retrofits, custom colors/transparency for BIPV, small-format panels — segments where the giants' 500 W glass slabs don't fit and per-watt price is not the buying criterion.
  • Lead time and service: weeks-not-months delivery, local warranty honored by a company that physically exists here, replacement singles (importers hate shipping one panel).
  • Assembler-as-brand for installers: white-label for PH installer networks who want a differentiated "Philippine-made" line — the sales channel is installers and distributors, not homeowners directly.
  • Incentive-adjusted domestic pricing: RE-Act VAT-free inputs + 7-yr ITH (§3) narrows — not closes — the import gap.
  • Export arbitrage UNVERIFIED: US tariff actions have hit Chinese and (2024–25) Vietnamese/Thai/Malaysian/Cambodian modules; whether Philippine-assembled modules currently enjoy a tariff advantage — and the non-circumvention rules on Chinese cells — changes quarter to quarter. This was in the interrupted research scope; re-verify before building any plan on it.

Strategic bottom line: stages 1–2 are justified by learning, certification, and the niche/service market — a real but modest business. Stage 3 at commodity scale only makes sense with either a locked distribution channel (utility/government/installer-network offtake) or a verified export-tariff advantage. Let the stage-2 sales data make that decision, not enthusiasm.
